Conclusion

What have been done?

On the basis of BDI and Kripke models specified in 'Problem Description' section a model has been built. This model consists of two main parts:

  • Civilization model. This part describes a model used to represent a civilization. Each civilization consists of a number of basic parameters:
    • Name. A civilization chosen name.
    • Attitude. A general outlook towards other societies in space. Malevolent civilizations generally strive to destroy civilizations they encounter, while benevolent ones are much more likely to try and make contact with alien society.
    • Technical level. A real number in range 0-10. This number represents technical prowess of the population. It being a real number constitutes subtle difference even between civilizations of the same scale. During an armed conflict this parameter defines who wins and who dies.
    • Depth of suspicion. An integer number in range 0-7. Represents societal arguments about other civilization's attitude. Is it worth making contact, if another society may be malevolent? Or, maybe, if another society is benevolent, but thinks that we are malevolent and may try to destroy us anyway? During the lifetime of a benevolent civilization this parameter defines its chances to make a preemptive strike, to hide ot to make contact.
    • State automaton. A representation of BDI model. Possible actions to take at any given point in time. Slightly different for different types of civilizations. For example, a malevolent society does not have an opportunity to make contact, while benevolent civilization does not consider a possibility of outright extermination of another society.
  • Statistics gathering engine. This engine is based on running a number of encounters between two civilizations. By the end of the encounter each of two civilizations either defended itself by destroying other civilization and survived alone, prevailed and destroyed the other one (hence also surviving alone), hid and thus removed a possibility of destruction or cooperated with other one. We provide a command-line based text interface that allows user to:
    • Make a manual run of one particular encounter simulation.
    • Make an interactive run of one particular encounter assuming a role of one of the societies participating in an encounter
    • Make a user-specified number of runs and gather a statistics

Statistics and analysis

We ran a number of simulated encounters to gather a statistics. Encounters were executed one by one, without taking into account previous experiences. As the focus of this project is a resolution of single encounter we were more interested in more secluded environments

First of all it is evident that approximately 74.12% (741221 out of 1000000) of all encounters end with a destruction of one of participating civilizations. This number is bad news for any society trying to make contact in Big Universe. Approximately two thirds of those are deaths of malevolent civilizations. This is understandable, as malevolents always go for an armed confrontation and have a 50% chance of having a higher tech level. Benevolents only make preemptive strikes or try to defend themselves. When they are trying to defend they again have a 50% chance of succeeding. Other deaths can be explained by preemptive strikes.

We distinguish a number of possible strategies for each civilization:

  • Destruction. "Destroy or be destroyed" as they say. This is a way that only malevolent civilizations take. Destruction of another civilization as soon as it is detected. Though it should be taken into account that sometimes another civilizations may prove quite resourceful, hence in 16.27% of encounters benevolent civilization manages to repel assault
  • Preemptive strikes. Sometimes event benevolent civilizations may be forced to take action. If one expects another one to strike first it may try to make a preemptive strike to be even faster. This kind of action was initiated in 4.832% of benevolent-benevolent encounters.
  • Stealth. Go into hiding is always an option for any civilization. If one just sits put and tries to do everything to stay undetected - it may actually survive. This accounts for 13.69% of all encounters in this particular mode
  • Cooperation. The ultimate goal of any benevolent society. This is dangerous as it implies that this society trusts another one with info about its location and state. This outcome constitutes 12.18% of encounters

It should also be noted that depth of suspicion has great influence over preferred course of actions of a benevolent civilization. The higher it is the more likely civilizations is to regard another one as being benevolent in nature and hence the more likely it is to make contact.
